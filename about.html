Teaching Philosophy

Economics is not a collection of facts to be memorised — it is a lens through which to understand the world. Every concept, from supply and demand to fiscal policy, connects to something real: a news headline, a government decision, a price on a shelf. My sessions are built around that connection.

Over a decade of teaching, combined with years of examining IB papers, has given me a precise understanding of where students lose marks and why. The gap between a 5 and a 7 is rarely about intelligence. It is almost always about knowing exactly how to structure an argument, which diagrams to draw, and which real-world examples to deploy. I teach that knowledge directly.

Every student I work with gets a tutor who has read thousands of exam scripts. That perspective is not something you can find in a textbook or a YouTube video. It is the core of what Pareto offers.

The Pareto Approach

Vilfredo Pareto observed that 80% of outcomes come from 20% of causes. In exam preparation, this is strikingly true: a small number of skills — diagram precision, command term awareness, real-world application — account for the majority of marks available. My sessions focus relentlessly on that 20%. No wasted time, no vague revision. Just the knowledge that moves the needle.
